# Platform Constitution

**Resolution:** 002  
**Status:** Adopted  
**Version:** 0.0.0  
**Release:** Constitutional Foundation  
**Adopted:** 20 July 2026  

---

## Purpose

This Constitution establishes the principles, constraints, and authority that
govern the Intelligent Decision-Making Platform.

All implementation and operation must remain consistent with these Articles.

## Article I — Mission

The platform exists to improve evidence-driven decision-making while
preserving explainability, accountability, governance, capital, operational
integrity, and validated learning.

Its first application is trading, but its architecture shall remain
domain-independent.

## Article II — Evidence Before Conclusions

Important conclusions must be supported by traceable evidence.

The platform must distinguish:

- observation from interpretation;
- evidence from opinion;
- assessment from recommendation;
- recommendation from permission;
- outcome from decision quality.

Material contradictory evidence must be preserved.

## Article III — Explainability

The platform must be able to reconstruct important decisions.

Explainability requires preservation of:

- source observations;
- evidence;
- assumptions;
- uncertainty;
- assessments;
- alternatives;
- recommendations;
- plans;
- governance decisions;
- execution facts;
- reviews;
- knowledge provenance.

A result that cannot be meaningfully explained must not be granted
unrestricted authority.

## Article IV — Separation of Responsibilities

Observation, research, assessment, decision synthesis, planning, governance,
execution, review, and knowledge validation are distinct responsibilities.

No subsystem may silently assume the authority of another.

In particular:

- observation systems do not recommend action;
- intelligence modules do not authorize execution;
- decision intelligence does not execute;
- planners do not approve their own plans;
- governance does not rewrite analysis;
- execution does not invent strategic intent;
- review does not rewrite history.

## Article V — Governance Before Automation

Automation is permitted only within explicit authority.

No action may be executed without a valid Governance Decision.

Automation may increase gradually, but governance, traceability, and
accountability must remain intact.

## Article VI — Capital Preservation

Capital preservation is the highest operational priority of the trading
application.

The platform must protect against:

- excessive position size;
- uncontrolled leverage;
- concentration;
- repeated impulsive entries;
- revenge trading;
- martingale behavior;
- overtrading;
- emotional early exits;
- breaches of loss limits;
- execution during unsafe system conditions.

A potentially profitable action may still be rejected when it violates risk
or governance policy.

## Article VII — Human Responsibility

The platform supports human judgment but does not remove human
responsibility.

The human retains responsibility for:

- defining goals;
- approving policies;
- reviewing architecture;
- evaluating performance;
- authorizing changes in automation;
- accepting the consequences of delegated authority.

The platform must not present itself as certain, infallible, or guaranteed to
be profitable.

## Article VIII — Incremental Development

The platform shall be built through small, testable capabilities.

Every milestone must leave the platform:

- working;
- testable;
- understandable;
- documented;
- reviewable.

Large unvalidated leaps and premature live deployment are prohibited by
engineering policy.

## Article IX — Continuous Learning

The platform must learn through structured review rather than uncontrolled
self-modification.

Outcomes do not become Knowledge automatically.

Candidate lessons require:

- provenance;
- review;
- validation;
- versioning;
- future reassessment.

## Article X — Transparency

Important actions, failures, overrides, and policy decisions must be logged
and traceable.

The platform must not conceal:

- uncertainty;
- missing data;
- model failure;
- policy rejection;
- execution deviation;
- contradictory evidence;
- unsuccessful outcomes.

## Article XI — Technology Independence

The platform's identity and core architecture shall remain independent of
specific technologies.

External systems shall be integrated through connectors and adapters.

No vendor, exchange, API, data format, programming language, database, or
model provider may become the platform's architectural center.

## Article XII — Quality

Production implementation shall favor:

- readable Python;
- strong typing;
- clear interfaces;
- focused responsibilities;
- validation;
- error handling;
- logging;
- unit tests;
- integration tests;
- documentation;
- deterministic behavior where practical.

Cleverness must not take priority over clarity.

## Article XIII — Evolution

The Constitution may evolve, but not silently.

A constitutional change requires formal review, documented reasoning, impact
analysis, version control, and approval.

Historical versions must remain available.

## Article XIV — Educational Integrity

The project is both a production-oriented platform and a long-term learning
project.

Implementation should help the developer understand:

- Python;
- software architecture;
- domain modeling;
- testing;
- risk management;
- execution systems;
- decision theory;
- governance;
- review and learning.

The project must not become a black box that the developer can operate but
cannot explain.

## Constitutional Priority

When principles compete, the following ordering applies unless a formally
approved policy states otherwise:

1. safety and capital preservation;
2. governance and legal or operational compliance;
3. data integrity and system correctness;
4. explainability and traceability;
5. decision quality;
6. execution efficiency;
7. profitability and optimization.
